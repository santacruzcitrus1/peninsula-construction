California ADU Laws in 2026:
What Every Homeowner Needs to Know

By Richard Rodriguez  |  June 2026  |  8 min read

California has passed more ADU-friendly legislation in the last 6 years than in the previous 30 combined. If you've looked into building an ADU before and were told "no" — it's worth looking again. The rules have changed dramatically in your favor.

Here's what the key laws actually mean for homeowners in Santa Cruz and Monterey Counties.

The Laws That Changed Everything

AB 68 (2020) — The Game Changer

This was the law that unlocked ADUs statewide. Key provisions: cities and counties must allow at least one ADU and one JADU on every single-family lot. They cannot require owner-occupancy as a condition of building. Minimum size standards cannot be used to effectively prohibit ADUs. This overrode decades of local restrictions overnight.

AB 2221 (2023) — Speed & Size

Cities must approve or deny ADU permits within 60 days. ADUs can be up to 850 sq ft (studio/1BR) or 1,000 sq ft (2BR+) regardless of local zoning. Setback requirements for ADUs converted from existing structures are capped at 4 feet from rear and side property lines.

SB 9 (2022) — Lot Splitting

Homeowners can now split their single-family lot into two parcels and build up to 2 units on each — potentially 4 units total where only 1 existed before. This is a major opportunity for larger properties, especially for real estate investors.

AB 1033 (2024) — ADU Condominiums

ADUs can now be sold separately from the primary home as condominiums. This creates a new exit strategy: build an ADU, collect rent, then sell it independently when property values peak. Massive for investors.

What Cities CANNOT Do

  • Require owner-occupancy on properties with ADUs (until 2025 — check current status)
  • Deny ADU permits based on parking requirements in most cases
  • Charge impact fees on ADUs under 750 sq ft
  • Apply design standards that would effectively prohibit ADU construction
  • Require replacement parking when a garage is converted to an ADU

What This Means for You

If you own a single-family home in Santa Cruz or Monterey Counties, California law gives you strong protections and clear rights to build an ADU. The question isn't whether you can — in most cases, you can. The question is what type makes the most sense for your property, your goals, and your budget.
